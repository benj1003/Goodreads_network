Network of Book Recommendations

Ever finished the greatest book ever and wondered “what’s next?”

Well sure you have - we all have. And next step could be to simply look at what other readers of your "greatest book ever" also read. But how are books related and recommended with each other? This is what we have investigated!

By creating a network of books, where the books are linked by its recommendations, we have investigated the properties of the network and answered questions such as: does links between a book and its related books end up creating a universe where few books are very influential? Does it keep readers within the same genre? Or maybe no patterns are present?

I’ve only heard bad about this book, but it has a very high rating, how can this be?

You sometimes run into books that are simply horrible, but its rating is very high and you start questioning yourself: Does the rating really reflect the readers actual opinion – who knows maybe it’s just a computer program that gives thousands of 5-star ratings to specific books. Under the hypothesis that people’s true opinions are reflected in the comments we have investigated how well the rating reflect people’s opinions. We further investigate if other quantitative measures provide a better estimate of book quality or if you really must sit down and read the comments.

On this page you can explore our main findings from the investigation on how one book is related with the books that are recommended on Goodreads, an online database of books. Under 'About' you will get a sense of the dataset used for this analysis showing all the descriptive statistics. We have investigated if the list of recommendations creates a network, where the same book is recommended all the time making some books highly influential in the literary society. We have further investigated if books are mainly recommended for other books within the same genre, which could limit readers to one sub-category of books. Read about this under the section 'Network'.

In addition to investigating the relationsship between books, we have investigated how correlated a books rating is, with the sentiment score of the book reviews. Our hypothesis was that highly rated books have a higher sentiment score in the reviews than a poorly rated book. The network analysis and sentiment analysis is tied together when investigating whether the most influential books in the network are also some of the books with highest sentiment or if it is something else that drives the high degree nodes. Read about this under the section 'Sentiment'.

Network

We have created a network, where each of the books from the positive list is a node and where two nodes are connected if one books has the other under the section 'Readers also enjoyed'. We have created the network as a directed network. This means that a node is only connected to the nodes it recomends - not the nodes that recomends that node.

We hereby get a network of 1,917 nodes and 10,001 edges.

With the network we investigate the degree distribution, which basically means that we investigate how many times each book has been recomended. The node with the highest degree (and thereby is the most recomended) is "Harry Potter and the Deathly Hallows" with degree 535. This means that "Harry Potter and the Deathly Hallows" is recommended for 25.95% of the books on the list. The average degree is 10.43, and thus it is 10 times more likely that Harry Potter is recommended than an average book.

The figure underneath illustrates the degree distribution for all the books in the positive list. It illustrates that few books have a very large degree while the majority of the books have a degree under 50. This indicates that there are a few select books that gets recommended for almost every other book on the list.

Other than degree, we also have other ways to quantify how influential a node is to the network. We have investigated the network with regards to betweenness centrality and eigenvector centrality. Betweenness centrality is a meassure of how many shortest paths passses through a given node. For our network a high betweenness centrality would indicate a gateway book, meaning if you read book A you may not be recommended book B, but you get recommended book C. Book C will then recommend you book B, and the only reason you read book B is therefore because you also read book C. This makes book C very influential. Eigenvector centrality is also an indicator for how influential a node is. A node gets a high score if its neighboring node also have a high score. This would for our network mean that a node with a high eigenvector centrality is being recomended by other books that many other books recomend. A book with high degree but low eigenvector centrality could therefore be a book that is recommended alot, but the books recommending it are not themselves recommended often.

Communities based on genre

We have now seen that some books does get recomended much more than the average book and therefore could be classified as highly influential in the literary society. But now lets turn our attention to investigate if the network contains clear groups - in network theory called communities. Our hypothesis was that a book is more likely to be recomended by another book if they are in the same genre, and thus each genre would be a small community in the network.

From the list of books we have 66 different genres. However as mentionedmany of the genres consists of very few books. We have therefore only considered the 15 largest and collected the books in the others in a new genre called "Miscellaneous".

With the updated genrelist, we have investigated how well our hyphothesis holds using a modularity score. This is a score ranging from -1 to 1 indicating how well the hypothesized communities, actually are small communities in the network. The closer to 1 the better the partition is.

Partitioning on genre the modularity score for the network is XXX .

This is considered a high score, but to actually tell something about how good partitioning on genre is, you must investigate how well it is possible to partition your network. There are several algorithms that does this. One of them is the louvrain algorithm, which based on the network identifies the best fitting communities.

From the confusion matrix we see that the algorithm does output communities that are consistent with the genres and thus genre seems like a good representative of communities, considering the network. We do, however, see that the modularity score is increased a lot (froom XXX to XXX) when using the Louvrain algorithm, which suggest that some influencial nodes don't represent their genre in the optimal communities..

We remind our self that the network consists nodes with very high degree, which may influence the results heavily, as these most likely are recommended by books from all genres. We investigate if this is the case by considering a subgraph, where the nodes in the top 1 percentile degree are removed.

On this network it is very clear to se that there are communities based on genre, which is supported by an increased modularity. To say that books generally recommends books within the same genre and therefore to some extend limits readers within a sub-category, is not wrong.

Text Analysis

On Goodreads users are able to write reviews on the books and many do. We have investigated these rewievs using sentiment analysis and further investigated the word prevalence of those classified as very positive and very negative respectively. Comparing each books sentiment score with the ratings we have investigated how well these match.

In the text analysis, we have included the books from the negative book list. The analysis therefore consists of books both with very high average rating and very low average rating.

Sentiment

Sentiment in text is the amount of emotion portrayed. Two methods can be used to calculate the sentiment of a text: a model can be trained on positive and negative data to learn nuances differentiating the two, or a rule-based approach can be used, where sentiment of a text is the result of a set of manually set rules. Often it is the rule-based approach that is used, as building a model to score automatically is both time-consuming and requires excessive amounts of data. The rule-based approach utilizes many Natural Language Processing methods such as tokenizing, stemming and part-of-speech (POS) tagging. The polarization of words is often the result of a look-up in a predefined lexical with polarity scores for certain words. For this project we have used VADER (Valence Aware Dictionary and sentiment Reasoner) sentiment package in Python. The VADER library is a rule-based approach especially appropriate for text from social media. It is a highly sophisticated package which takes the syntax of the sentence into account when scoring and thereby avoiding a common fall-back of the classical lexical approach, such as the incorporations of negations. Further, VADER has a sense of capitalization making words such as "GREAT" score higher than "great" as well as a sense of punctuation, letting multiple exclamation marks give a higher polarity score. The package is tuned for sentence-based sentiment but will for this purpose be used on full reviews for a single book some spanning several paragraphs. The final sentiment for the book is then the mean of the sentiment of the reviews for the book. The output from the VADER analysis is a negative, neutral, positive and compound score, where the compound score is the overall sentiment of the sentence and what is used in this analysis. The scores can range between -1 to 1 with 1 being the most positive and -1 the most negative.

Below you see the distribution of the sentiment score for the books from the postive and negative list respectively.

As expected we see that the books from the negative list generally scores lower than the books from the positive list. However, from the illustration we also see that some books from the negative list scores worse than the average 'bad' book, and some of the books from the positive list scores higher than the average 'good' book.

We therefore introduce two new groupd, where books are classified as very negative and very positive as the books that scored below the 5th percentile and above the 95th percentile respectively of the combined sentiment scores for all books.

With the information retrieval method, TD-IDF (term frequency–inverse document frequency) we have investigated the word prevalence for the very negative and very positive books. The TD-IDF score is calculated as the frequency a word is present in a document (here all the reviews in one group) adjusted with how used the word is across all documents in corpus. This concludes the major investigations of sentiment in the reviews. The extreme cases of sentiment catch reviews that are highly polar but creating TFIDF conditioned on overall sentiment did not appear to give useful divisions of the two groups. The hypothesis was that calculating the TFIDF of the positively scored books would highlight very positive words, as these are more likely in that group. The conclusion is though, that the words caught by the TFIDF seem to be more differentiated than that.

The general idea was that using sentiment analysis the rating of a books could be somewhat predicted, as sentiment analysis on other kind of reviews have proven very effective. Our results could indicate that this approach is made difficult when the object of the review is text as well. In this case the reviews often contain quotes are descriptions of the books, and if the book itself carry a low sentiment, the review will score likewise even though the reviewer expressed a positive opinion about the books.

Summary

Throughout this project a network of books has been explored. Looking at books from a list of very popular books a network was created as a link between two books if one book was recommended on the other books Goodreads page.
The resulting network showed that few books have very large degree and are therefore recommended often by others. Investigating the network with betweenness-centrality equally showed that some books seem very influential. The eigenvector-centrality scores though contradicted this. However, it still seems clear that some books in the network are very influential.
The next approach to investigating the network we used was community detection. One hypothesis was that books recommend books within the same genre. Here it was seen that when communities were partitioned on genre in the full graph did indicate our hypothesis was true, but it also indicated that some very influential nodes don't represent their genre in the optimal communities. We therefore created a sub-graph, where the nodes in the top 1 percentile degree was removed. This even further indicated that books do tend to recommend books within the same genre more than books of different genres.

Next, the reviews of each book were taken into account. To get two groups to compare, books from a very poorly rated list of books was also scraped. First, a TFIDF analysis was performed between two documents; one with all the tokens from the reviews on the positive list, and one with the tokens from the reviews on the negative list.
The result was illustrated using word clouds. The intuition was, that were more prominent in the positive list than the negative list would be very positive words and opposite for words originating from the negative list. This was not the case. Instead, the analysis seemed to pick up on highly book specific words indicating that descriptive polar words where present in both documents.
Since the two groups differed as being highly rated versus low rated books, a clear distinction on sentiment in the reviews was also expected. Finding the sentiment of each reviews and meaning over the 15 reviews acquired for each book did show a separation of distribution of sentiment for the two groups.
It did also indicate overlapping sentiment, meaning that some books from the positive list scored a rather negative sentiment and some books from the negative list scored positively.
This led to the creation of two new groups, where a threshold for high and low sentiment respectively was set, and all books regardless of origin scoring above or below these thresholds were classified as either positive book or negative book.
Diving into the most positive and negative rated books and investigating their sentiment in reviews showed that the sentiment analysis had in fact managed to pick up the general sentiment of a review. A TFIDF analysis was once more performed on this division of books, and a better intuitive wordclouds appeared. They were though still very book specific and some of the highest scoring words referred to specific authors or characters.

Lastly, an investigation of correlations between some of the parameters was performed. This indicated a slight positive correlation between the rating of a book and the sentiment score of their reviews. It was though not as prevalent as first hypothesized.
No correlation was found between the degree and the sentiment of the reviews for a book, dismissing the idea that if a book is recommended often, then the sentiment in the reviews is also high.
Last correlation investigated was a correlation between rating and the logarithm of the rating count. Here a clear positive correlation was seen indicating a high rating count gives a high rating. The data is though biased since the books chosen are very popular books conditioning them to have both a higher rating and a higher rating count.

Overall, the analysis performed in this project showed, that few books are recommended often, making them very influential in the literary society. Books recommended by these books therefore hold a higher influence themselves. Further, it was showed that TFIDF and sentiment analysis on reviews of books, was skewed by the fact that the reviews themselves often contain quotes or descriptions of the book reviewed.
